reflection recon — the added stages

Sub-pixel registration. The eye jitters between frames, so naïve averaging smears detail. Before each frame joins the stack it's aligned to the first (reference) frame by translational cross-correlation on a downsampled luma copy, refined to sub-pixel with a parabolic fit on the correlation peak, then resampled with bilinear interpolation. This is the same "shift-and-add" that astrophotographers use to beat the noise floor. It corrects translation only — not rotation, scale, or the corneal warp — so keep the subject roughly still.

Richardson–Lucy deconvolution. An iterative maximum-likelihood deblur assuming Poisson noise: u ← u · [ (d / (u∗p)) ∗ p ], repeated. Here p is a Gaussian standing in for defocus blur (set its radius to your out-of-focus disc). RL amplifies noise, so run it after integration has cleaned the frame, and stop before ringing appears — more iterations is not better. The corneal PSF is really space-varying, so this is an approximation, but it's the honest, standard tool.

sensor forensics — the correct PRNU method

Every sensor imprints photo-response non-uniformity: a fixed, per-pixel gain pattern from silicon manufacturing variation. It's multiplicative and unique per physical device — a fingerprint. The canonical pipeline (Lukáš–Fridrich–Goljan, 2006):

  1. Residual. For each frame I, denoise and take W = I − F(I). Production uses a wavelet denoiser; this bench uses a Gaussian high-pass as a fast proxy.
  2. ML fingerprint. Because PRNU scales with brightness, estimate K̂ = Σ(Wᵢ·Iᵢ) / Σ(Iᵢ²) over many flat bright frames.
  3. Clean it. Zero-mean every row and column to strip shared CFA/demosaic and row-column artifacts, or different cameras of the same model false-match.
  4. Detect. For a test frame, correlate its residual against the predicted signal I·K̂. Report NCC, and a PCE (peak-to-correlation-energy) statistic — peak² over the energy of off-peak shifts — which thresholds cleanly.

This tool simplifies real forensics: it works on already-compressed 8-bit webcam frames (RAW is far better), uses a Gaussian denoiser instead of wavelet, and computes a PCE approximation over decoy shifts rather than a full FFT correlation surface. It teaches the method faithfully; it is not a courtroom pipeline.

where this technology actually gets used in research

Reflection / inverse rendering

  • Privacy attacks on video calls — recovering on-screen text from eyeglass reflections (Long et al., Private Eye, 2023) and screen/room reconstruction from the eye (Backes et al., Reflections of the Invisible, IEEE S&P 2008–2010).
  • Eye tracking & gaze — corneal-reflection (Purkinje / "glint") tracking is the basis of most commercial eye trackers.
  • Environment capture for graphics/AR — eyes, chrome spheres, and other specular surfaces act as light probes to recover the surrounding illumination (Debevec's light-probe work; "the world in an eye," Nishino & Nayar 2004).
  • Biometrics & liveness — screen reflections in the cornea help distinguish a live face from a printed photo or replayed video in anti-spoofing.
  • Ophthalmology — corneal topography and keratometry read the shape of reflected ring patterns off the tear film.

Sensor / camera forensics

  • Source camera identification — tying a photo or clip to one specific device via PRNU.
  • Tamper / splice localization — a forged region won't correlate with the fingerprint, so absence of PRNU maps the manipulation.
  • Deepfake & synthetic-media detection — generated frames lack a consistent physical PRNU, and GAN pipelines leave their own spectral fingerprints.
  • Device clustering / provenance — grouping images by unknown source camera across a large set, used in investigations of illicit-media networks.
  • Integrity for evidence — verifying a body-cam or dashcam clip came from the claimed hardware and wasn't re-encoded through another device.

Dual-use note. Reflection recovery is simultaneously a privacy attack and the reason to study it defensively (blur backgrounds, angle screens away, treat glasses on camera as a leak). PRNU is a forensic defense that is also a de-anonymization vector — the same fingerprint that convicts a forger can link a whistleblower's photos across accounts. Worth holding both edges in mind.
